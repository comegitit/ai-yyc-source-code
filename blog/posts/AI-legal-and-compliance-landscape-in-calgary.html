The Legal and Compliance Space in Calgary's AI Landscape: Past, Present, and Future

May 27, 2026 · Governance & Ethics · AI Governance, Legal & Compliance, Alberta, Digital Strategy · 4 Minute Read

Canada was the first country in the world to launch a national AI strategy. That was 2017. Nearly a decade later, Calgary organizations are deploying AI at scale (in energy operations, financial services, legal practice, and beyond) without a single dedicated AI law on the books at any level of government. Understanding how we arrived here, and what comes next, is not a legal exercise. It is a strategic one.

Building Without a Blueprint: 2016–2021

In the mid-2010s, the regulatory conversation around AI in Canada was largely theoretical. The federal Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA), written in 2000, was the closest thing to a governing framework for organizations deploying data-driven systems. It was designed for a world of transactional e-commerce, not autonomous decision-making at enterprise scale.

Alberta operated under its own private-sector privacy statute, the Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA), which provided broadly similar protections and gave the province's Information and Privacy Commissioner (OIPC) meaningful investigative authority. For Calgary's energy and financial services sectors, both laws created compliance obligations around data collection and consent, but offered no specific guidance on algorithmic systems or automated decisions.

The federal government signalled ambition in March 2017 with a $125 million investment to launch the Pan-Canadian Artificial Intelligence Strategy, the first of its kind globally. The strategy established three national AI institutes: Mila in Montreal, the Vector Institute in Toronto, and the Alberta Machine Intelligence Institute (Amii) in Edmonton. It was a research and talent play. The governance architecture to support commercialization of those capabilities was not yet on the agenda.

The gap between innovation pace and regulatory clarity widened through this period. Canada's Digital Charter, introduced in 2019, articulated principles around trust and data sovereignty, but principles are not law. Organizations moving quickly on AI adoption, including those in Calgary's oil and gas and financial services corridors, were largely self-governing, guided by internal ethics frameworks, contractual obligations, and the weight of reputational risk.

The Attempt at a Framework: Bill C-27 and AIDA, 2022–2025

In June 2022, the federal government tabled Bill C-27, the Digital Charter Implementation Act. Bundled within it was the Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), Canada's first attempt at a comprehensive national AI regulation. Taking a risk-tiered approach broadly aligned with the EU's emerging AI Act, AIDA would have required developers, deployers, and operators of high-impact AI systems to conduct risk assessments, maintain documentation, and disclose key system information. Enforcement would fall to a new AI and Data Commissioner.

The bill spent over two years in legislative review. Industry raised concerns about definitional ambiguity. Civil society and academic voices pushed back on governance structure, arguing that positioning the Commissioner under the Minister responsible for promoting industry created a structural conflict. Proposed amendments arrived late in 2023. Clause-by-clause review began in spring 2024 and was paused by summer. Then, on January 6, 2025, Prime Minister Trudeau prorogued Parliament. Bill C-27 died on the Order Paper, taking AIDA with it.

The result: Canada entered 2025 still governed by a privacy law written before the first iPhone existed, with no federal AI statute in force or imminent. In June 2025, Canada's first Minister of Artificial Intelligence and Digital Innovation, Evan Solomon, appointed by Prime Minister Carney in May 2025, confirmed that AIDA would not be reintroduced in its original form, signalling a preference for a more targeted, sector-sensitive approach he described publicly as "light, tight, and right." A successor bill is widely expected to be tabled in 2026, though no official draft has yet been released.

The Provincial Response: Alberta Steps Into the Vacuum

With federal momentum stalled, Alberta's regulatory posture has grown notably more active. In December 2024, the province launched its AI Data Centre Strategy, articulating an ambition to attract $100 billion in private investment and position Alberta as North America's premier data centre destination, leveraging abundant energy capacity, cold climate, and a competitive fiscal environment. That strategy was accompanied by a levy framework to ensure Albertans share in the economic upside.

On the governance side, the picture has been equally consequential. Alberta's legacy public-sector privacy statute, FOIP, was repealed and replaced on June 11, 2025, by two new instruments: the Access to Information Act and the Protection of Privacy Act (POPA). POPA includes early provisions around automated decision-making transparency for public bodies, but the OIPC has noted explicitly that the legislation fell short of its AI-specific recommendations.

In August 2025, the OIPC published a landmark report calling for a standalone provincial AI law to complement modernized privacy statutes, one calibrated to Alberta's industries and values, and aligned with international governance standards. The Commissioner's office proposed that such a framework should govern the collection of personal information to train AI systems, and codify rights for individuals where AI influences consequential decisions. Minister Glubish acknowledged the report and signalled openness to exploring the regulatory path forward.

The same month, the Alberta Court of King's Bench delivered a significant ruling in Clearview AI Inc. v. Alberta (Information and Privacy Commissioner), upholding an OIPC order directing the facial recognition company to cease operations and delete Albertans' data, while simultaneously finding portions of PIPA to be constitutionally problematic in their treatment of publicly available information. The ruling signalled both that Alberta's regulators are prepared to act, and that the province's legislative framework requires modernization to meet the moment.

The Present: Operating in Productive Ambiguity

As of mid-2026, the legal landscape for Calgary organizations deploying AI remains a patchwork. PIPEDA governs at the federal level for private-sector activity, with no AI-specific overlay. Alberta's PIPA is under active reform. The OIPC's recommended AI statute has not yet been introduced. Quebec remains the only Canadian province with binding automated decision-making obligations under its private-sector privacy legislation.

In the absence of domestic clarity, Calgary's legal and compliance community has increasingly looked outward. Major firms are treating GDPR-level practices as a de facto internal standard. The Law Society of Alberta has published a substantive playbook on generative AI use for legal practitioners, covering professional responsibility, confidentiality obligations, and risk management. Alberta lawyers now have clearer guidance on the ethics of AI adoption in legal practice than most organizations have on AI use in general enterprise contexts.

What the Next Five Years Are Likely to Bring

Looking to 2031, the trajectory is toward increasing regulatory density, but the path will be uneven. Several developments are reasonably foreseeable.

At the federal level, a successor to AIDA is widely expected to be tabled in 2026, Minister Solomon has confirmed a new regulatory initiative is coming, one deliberately distinct from its predecessor. It will likely be narrower and more sector-focused than AIDA was designed to be. Financial services, healthcare, and critical infrastructure, all sectors with material operations in Calgary, are the most probable initial targets of federal oversight. Organizations in those industries that begin building governance infrastructure now will face considerably less friction when compliance obligations arrive than those that wait.

Alberta is likely to move toward standalone AI legislation, informed by the OIPC's 2025 report and by the province's strategic interest in leading Canada's data centre build-out. A provincial law that provides clarity for both operators of AI infrastructure and organizations deploying AI in regulated industries would serve Alberta's competitive positioning as much as its privacy interests. The two goals are not in tension; they are mutually reinforcing.

The energy sector's AI trajectory will also create novel compliance terrain. As Calgary-based operators deploy AI for predictive maintenance, reservoir modelling, and emissions monitoring, questions around explainability, auditability, and liability for automated decisions will arrive faster than legislation does. The organizations that treat these questions as engineering problems rather than legal ones, embedding governance into system design rather than appending compliance reviews after the fact, will have a meaningful advantage.

The Executive Lens

The practical conclusion for Calgary organizations is straightforward, even if the regulatory landscape is not: governance cannot wait for legislation to mature. The organizations that will navigate the next wave of AI compliance well are not those that have tracked every parliamentary bulletin. They are the ones that have built internal clarity on what AI systems they operate, what decisions those systems influence, what data they consume, and who is accountable when outcomes diverge from intent.

That work is not primarily legal. It is operational, cultural, and strategic. The legal framework will arrive in time. The question is whether your organization's AI governance posture will be ready to meet it, or scrambling to catch up.
